# Module 3: Social Engineering

## Lesson 05: Fraud, Scams, and Influence Campaigns

---

# Overview

The internet has become a powerful platform for communication, commerce, and information sharing. Unfortunately, it is also used by cybercriminals and other bad actors to conduct fraud, scams, and influence campaigns.

Many of these attacks rely on social engineering techniques such as phishing, pretexting, impersonation, and malware to manipulate victims for financial, personal, or political gain.

This lesson explores cyber fraud, cyber scams, and online influence campaigns, along with common examples and attack methods.

---

# Learning Objectives

After completing this lesson, you should be able to:

* Describe cyber fraud.
* Describe cyber scams.
* Identify common examples of fraud and scams.
* Explain how online influence campaigns operate.
* Understand the risks posed by misinformation and disinformation campaigns.

---

# Cyber Fraud

## Definition

Cyber fraud is the use of deception, social engineering, malware, or other cyber techniques to obtain financial gain, personal gain, or unauthorized benefits.

Cyber fraud often involves sophisticated planning and can target:

* Individuals
* Businesses
* Governments
* Financial institutions

---

# Goals of Cyber Fraud

Attackers may seek to:

* Steal money
* Obtain sensitive information
* Commit identity theft
* Gain access to accounts
* Acquire confidential data
* Conduct espionage

---

# Cyber Fraud Characteristics

| Characteristic        | Description                    |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Intentional           | Deliberately planned           |
| Deceptive             | Relies on lies or manipulation |
| Financially Motivated | Often seeks monetary gain      |
| Technology Enabled    | Uses internet-based platforms  |
| Criminal Activity     | Illegal conduct                |

---

# Cyber Scams

## Definition

Cyber scams are deceptive schemes designed to trick victims into giving away money, information, or access.

Scams are generally considered less sophisticated or smaller in scale than major fraud operations, but they can still cause significant financial and emotional damage.

---

# Important Fact

According to the lesson content, elderly individuals are frequently targeted because:

* They may be more trusting.
* They often possess substantial savings.
* They may be less familiar with modern cyber threats.

---

# Cyber Fraud vs Cyber Scams

| Feature            | Cyber Fraud                | Cyber Scam                 |
| ------------------ | -------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Scale              | Often larger               | Often smaller              |
| Complexity         | More sophisticated         | Usually simpler            |
| Goal               | Financial or personal gain | Financial or personal gain |
| Legality           | Criminal                   | Criminal                   |
| Social Engineering | Commonly used              | Commonly used              |

---

# Common Attack Methods

Fraud and scams often use:

* Phishing
* Spear Phishing
* Whaling
* Smishing
* Vishing
* Malware
* Fake Websites
* Social Media Manipulation
* Identity Impersonation

---

# Examples of Cyber Fraud and Cyber Scams

---

# 1. Copycat Government Websites

## Definition

Attackers create fake government websites that resemble legitimate government services.

---

# Goal

Steal:

* User credentials
* Personal information
* Credit card information
* Banking information

---

# Typical Attack Flow

```text
Phishing Email
      ↓
Victim Clicks Link
      ↓
Fake Government Website
      ↓
Victim Enters Information
      ↓
Data Stolen
```

---

# Example

A victim receives an email claiming to be from a tax authority and is directed to a fake tax portal.

---

# 2. Dating and Romance Scams

## Definition

Attackers build online relationships to gain trust before requesting money or personal information.

---

# Common Platforms

* Dating websites
* Social media platforms
* Chat applications
* Messaging services

---

# Attack Process

```text
Create Fake Identity
        ↓
Build Emotional Connection
        ↓
Gain Trust
        ↓
Request Money
        ↓
Disappear
```

---

# Warning Signs

* Requests for money
* Refusal to meet in person
* Emotional pressure
* Emergency situations requiring funds

---

# 3. Holiday Fraud

## Definition

Attackers advertise vacations, hotels, accommodations, or travel packages that do not exist.

---

# Goal

Collect payment for fake services.

---

# Examples

* Fake hotel reservations
* Non-existent vacation rentals
* Fake airline promotions
* Fraudulent travel packages

---

# 4. Mandate Fraud

## Definition

Mandate fraud occurs when attackers intercept business communications and redirect payments.

---

# How It Works

1. Attacker compromises email communication.
2. Attacker observes financial transactions.
3. Attacker sends a fake invoice.
4. Invoice contains attacker's bank details.
5. Victim unknowingly transfers money.

---

# Attack Flow

```text
Customer ↔ Vendor
       ↓
Attacker Intercepts Communication
       ↓
Fake Invoice Sent
       ↓
Money Sent to Attacker
```

---

# Why It Is Dangerous

The transaction appears legitimate because the attacker uses information from real business communications.

---

# 5. Pharming

## Definition

Pharming redirects users from legitimate websites to fraudulent websites without their knowledge.

---

# Common Targets

* Banking websites
* E-commerce platforms
* Government portals
* Payment services

---

# Goal

Steal:

* Usernames
* Passwords
* Financial information

---

# Difference Between Phishing and Pharming

| Phishing                      | Pharming                            |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Victim clicks malicious link  | Victim redirected automatically     |
| Relies heavily on user action | Often occurs without user awareness |
| Uses deceptive communication  | Uses redirection techniques         |

---

# 6. Greeting Card Scams

## Definition

Attackers send malicious electronic greeting cards.

---

# Common Occasions

* Birthdays
* Christmas
* Easter
* New Year
* Valentine's Day

---

# Goal

Convince victims to:

* Click links
* Download files
* Install malware

---

# Example

The birthday card attack used in the Emily Williams social engineering case.

---

# Summary of Common Fraud and Scam Types

| Attack Type                 | Primary Goal               |
| --------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Copycat Government Websites | Credential theft           |
| Romance Scams               | Financial fraud            |
| Holiday Fraud               | Payment theft              |
| Mandate Fraud               | Business payment diversion |
| Pharming                    | Credential theft           |
| Greeting Card Scam          | Malware delivery           |

# Online Influence Campaigns

## Definition

An online influence campaign is a coordinated effort to manipulate public opinion and promote a specific narrative.

---

# Purpose

Influence campaigns seek to:

* Change opinions
* Spread misinformation
* Promote disinformation
* Create social division
* Manipulate public behavior
* Influence political or social outcomes

---

# Common Participants

Influence campaigns may be conducted by:

* Criminal organizations
* Activist groups
* Political organizations
* Intelligence agencies
* Nation-state actors

---

# Why Influence Campaigns Are Effective

Social media enables information to spread rapidly.

A single false story can reach:

* Thousands of people
* Millions of people
* Entire countries

Within a very short period.

---

# Typical Influence Campaign Lifecycle

---

# Step 1: Create Fake Accounts

The attacker creates numerous fake social media profiles.

---

# Step 2: Create a Narrative

The attacker develops content supporting a desired message.

---

# Step 3: Publish Content

Fake accounts post content across multiple platforms.

---

# Step 4: Amplification

Real users begin sharing the content.

---

# Step 5: Viral Spread

The content reaches large audiences.

---

# Step 6: Mainstream Adoption

Traditional media outlets report on the story.

---

# Influence Campaign Flow

```text
Fake Accounts Created
          ↓
Narrative Developed
          ↓
Content Published
          ↓
Users Share Content
          ↓
Story Goes Viral
          ↓
Media Amplifies Message
```

---

# Why Attackers Use Influence Campaigns

Influence campaigns offer several advantages:

### Low Cost

Creating social media accounts is inexpensive.

---

### Large Reach

Content can reach millions of users.

---

### Anonymity

Attackers can hide their identity.

---

### Difficult Attribution

Identifying the true source can be challenging.

---

### Reduced Risk

Compared to direct attacks, influence operations are harder to prove and punish.

---

# Misinformation vs Disinformation

## Misinformation

False information shared without intent to deceive.

### Example

A person unknowingly shares inaccurate information.

---

## Disinformation

False information deliberately created and shared to deceive.

### Example

An attacker intentionally spreads fabricated claims.

---

# Restaurant Rival Example

## Scenario

Restaurant Owner A wants to harm Restaurant Owner B.

---

# Attack Process

1. Anonymous social media accounts are created.
2. False accusations are posted.
3. Social media users share the claims.
4. Public outrage grows.
5. Customers boycott the business.

---

# Challenge for the Victim

No matter how Restaurant Owner B responds:

* Staying silent may appear guilty.
* Denying the accusation may not stop the spread.
* Counteraccusations may worsen the situation.

---

# Key Lesson

False narratives can spread rapidly online and cause significant damage before facts are verified.

---

# Influence Campaign Indicators

Possible signs include:

* Large numbers of new accounts
* Coordinated posting activity
* Repeated messaging
* Emotionally charged content
* Lack of credible evidence
* Rapid viral spread

---

# Protection Against Fraud, Scams, and Influence Campaigns

---

# For Individuals

* Verify websites before entering information.
* Be skeptical of urgent requests.
* Confirm payment instructions independently.
* Avoid clicking unknown links.
* Verify online identities.
* Research offers before paying.

---

# For Organizations

* Train employees on social engineering.
* Verify financial transactions.
* Use multi-factor authentication (MFA).
* Monitor suspicious activity.
* Establish communication verification procedures.
* Conduct security awareness training.

---

# Key Terms

| Term               | Definition                                             |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| Cyber Fraud        | Deception used for financial or personal gain          |
| Cyber Scam         | Fraudulent scheme designed to trick victims            |
| Pharming           | Redirecting users to fake websites                     |
| Mandate Fraud      | Redirecting business payments through fake invoices    |
| Romance Scam       | Emotional manipulation for financial gain              |
| Influence Campaign | Organized effort to manipulate public opinion          |
| Misinformation     | False information shared unintentionally               |
| Disinformation     | False information shared intentionally                 |
| Narrative          | Story or message promoted during an influence campaign |

---

# Key Takeaways

* Cyber fraud uses deception to obtain financial or personal gain.
* Cyber scams are fraudulent schemes targeting individuals or organizations.
* Common scams include romance scams, holiday fraud, pharming, mandate fraud, and greeting card scams.
* Influence campaigns seek to manipulate public opinion on a large scale.
* Social media platforms are commonly used to spread narratives.
* Fake accounts often play a major role in influence operations.
* Misinformation is accidental; disinformation is intentional.
* Verification and security awareness are critical defenses.

### Q1: What is cyber fraud?

**Answer:** Deception used for financial or personal gain through cyber means.

**Answer:** A fraudulent scheme designed to trick victims into giving away money or information.

**Answer:** Redirecting users from a legitimate website to a fake one.

**Answer:** False information shared without intent to deceive.

### Q9: What is disinformation?

**Answer:** False information intentionally created to deceive others.

### Q10: What is usually the first step in an influence campaign?

**Answer:** Creating fake social media accounts.

# Summary

Cyber fraud and cyber scams use deception, social engineering, and technology to obtain money, information, or other benefits from victims. Common examples include copycat government websites, romance scams, holiday fraud, mandate fraud, pharming, and greeting card scams. Online influence campaigns are large-scale efforts to manipulate public opinion by spreading narratives through fake accounts and social media amplification. Understanding these threats and recognizing their warning signs helps individuals and organizations reduce the risk of becoming victims.
